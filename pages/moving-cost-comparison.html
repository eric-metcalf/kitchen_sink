A Cost Comparison

How much
to move everything
3,000 miles east?

Seven honest options for getting a household from the Bay Area to the Atlantic — what each one really costs in 2026, what you'll be doing yourself, and which to pick when.

The Route
SFO NYC
≈ 2,900 miles · 5–14 days transit
↓ The Short Answer

Cheapest is a U-Haul truck and a long drive. Easiest is full-service movers. The sweet spot is a moving container.

For a typical 1–2 bedroom move, expect roughly $2,500 driving a rental truck yourself, $3,500–$6,000 for a moving container like PODS or U-Pack, and $6,000–$12,000+ for full-service movers who pack, load, drive, and unload.

If your sister is willing to ditch the furniture and just ship boxes, she can do the whole move for under $1,000 via Greyhound's Busfreighter or LTL freight — and probably come out ahead even after buying a new couch on Craigslist when she lands.

§ 03

The box-only path, examined closely

Five ways to ship without a truck

The cheapest cross-country move is the one with no furniture. Sell the couch on Marketplace, donate the IKEA shelves, ship the rest. Total damage: $400–$1,500, often less than even a single moving container costs. But "cheap shipping" is genuinely harder than it was five years ago — the legendary Greyhound Package Express, the favorite of grad students and broke artists for a century, shut its doors in 2022. Here's what's actually still available in 2026.

A Safe & Boring

Parcel carriers

USPS · UPS · FedEx

$20 – $60 per box

The dependable choice. Five to fifteen boxes via USPS Priority Mail or FedEx Ground runs $200–$700 total, with tracking, insurance options, and 3–7 day transit. UPS and FedEx will pick up at her SF apartment for a small fee; USPS goes via any post office.

Don't overlook USPS Media Mail for books and CDs specifically — it's around $4–$15 per box and unbeatable for anyone with a real library.

Best for: fewer than 10 boxes, or anything she'd actually be sad to lose.

B Door-to-Door

Small-move specialists

MiniMoves · Ship Smart · College Boxes

$400 – $1,500

Curated brokers built specifically for partial-household moves — the gap between "few boxes via FedEx" and "full PODS container." They pick up at her SF apartment, coordinate the freight, and deliver to her NYC door.

College Boxes runs through the FedEx network (most reliable). Ship Smart and MiniMoves use a mix of LTL carriers and white-glove services. Pricing scales with weight and distance, but a typical 15-box cross-country move lands around $700–$1,200.

Best for: 10–30 boxes when she wants door-to-door without driving anywhere.

C DIY Freight

LTL freight (palletized)

via uShip · Estes · Old Dominion · ABF

$300 – $2,000

The commercial freight world will move household goods if you palletize them. Stack boxes on a 4'×4' wood pallet, shrink-wrap the whole thing into a single unit, and a less-than-truckload carrier will haul it cross-country for far less than parcel rates.

The catch: pallets get moved between trucks at regional terminals (more handling = more risk), and you'll need a forklift or pay for liftgate service on both ends. uShip's marketplace lets carriers bid against each other, which usually wins the best price.

Best for: 200–2,000 lbs of dense, durable items — books, dishes, sturdy gear.

D Cheapest, Caveat Emptor

Busfreighter

Bus-network broker

$150 – $600

The cheapest option still standing. Busfreighter is a third-party broker that arranges shipments across regional bus carriers — they used to ride on Greyhound, now it's a patchwork. Five 40-lb boxes cross-country has historically run $160–$260.

The trade-off is real: BBB rating of D-, mixed customer reviews, reports of damaged or delayed shipments, and you have to drop off and pick up at bus terminals (no door delivery in most cases). They don't offer meaningful insurance.

Use only for replaceable, non-fragile, non-valuable items — and only if budget is the absolute top priority.

↓ In Memoriam

Greyhound Package Express, 1929–2022. For nearly a century, the cheapest way to move a few boxes across America was on the Greyhound. The service officially shut down on September 30, 2022. If anyone — a blog post, an old comment thread, a friend who moved in 2019 — recommends it, they're working from outdated information. Busfreighter still uses parts of the bus network, but the original golden-era service is gone.

§ 05

Money-saving tactics, regardless of method

Seven things that actually move the needle
i.

Move October through April

Off-peak months run 15–25% cheaper. Movers are slow, prices drop, availability opens up. Avoid June–August at all costs.

ii.

Get three quotes, always

Especially for full-service movers — quotes can vary by $3,000–$5,000 for the same job. Insist on binding (not "non-binding") estimates.

iii.

Verify the FMCSA license

Interstate movers must register with the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration. Look up the USDOT number at fmcsa.dot.gov before signing.

iv.

Aggressively purge first

You pay by weight or volume. Every box you don't ship is $50–$100 saved. Books, in particular, are heavy and cheap to replace at the library.

v.

Mid-month, mid-week

The 1st, 15th, and last days of the month are the busiest for moves (lease turnover). Booking the 8th or 22nd, on a Tuesday, saves real money.

vi.

Reserve SF parking permits early

SFMTA temporary moving permits are required and run $50–$150. Without one, the truck or container can't legally park, and you'll pay shuttle fees ($300–$500).

vii.

Pack books in USPS Media Mail

If she has a serious book collection, USPS Media Mail is roughly $4 per box. Don't pay full freight rates to ship paperbacks across the country.
